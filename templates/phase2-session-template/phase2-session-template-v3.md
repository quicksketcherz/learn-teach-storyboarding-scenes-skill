## Before you start

Pick a **sequence** (not a whole film) — short enough to hold in memory after one watch. Around 30 to 90 seconds is the sweet spot. Bring it ready to play.

**Drawing surface — choose one:**

- **Option A — Ghibli 5-panel template** (printed or on tablet). Five landscape panels, INSIDE / CONTENTS columns for notes, TIME (SEC) column for shot duration. Print a fresh sheet for each pass.

- **Option B — Field notebook overlay technique** (no template needed). Use facing pages: page 1 stays as your reference. Draw 1st pass on page 2. For 2nd pass, flip to page 4 — page 3 acts as the overlay surface above page 1's reference. Keep stacking pages per pass. Note which shots live on which page.

*The principle is separate sheets per pass so you can see what you're refining against — not the specific tooling.*

## The idea

Phase 1 trained your eye on the frame. Phase 2 trains it on the **cut** — and on the habit of **iteration**.

You're not drawing a sequence once. You're drawing it three times — each pass refining what the last one caught.

**1st pass** is fast and rough; your Phase 1 instincts firing at speed. **2nd pass** pushes detail into the panels that are working and rescues the ones that aren't. **3rd pass** is focus work — using shadow, line weight, and contrast to direct the viewer's eye inside every frame. Every pass asks the same question at a different scale: how is the audience's attention being moved?

### 5 shots, not 5 panels

The exercise is to identify and draw **5 shots in sequence** — not to fill 5 boxes. A shot can span multiple panels: a wide establish might take 1 panel, a 3-character gesture beat might need 3. You're allocating panels to shots. **That allocation is the skill.**

*Phase 2 is about reading sequences. The panel count is a budget, not a target.*

---

## Sequence, scene, shot — a quick reminder

| Shot | One unbroken camera take. The smallest unit. |
|------|------------------------------------------------|
| Scene | A unit of action in one place and time — made of shots. |
| Sequence | A chain of scenes telling one chunk of story — the engine of Phase 2. |

*Analogy: a shot is a word, a scene is a sentence, a sequence is a paragraph. Phase 1 taught you to read words. Phase 2 teaches you to read paragraphs.*

*Example: in John Wick's nightclub scene, one shot is Wick reloading; a scene is the bathroom encounter; the sequence is the full raid from entrance to exit.*

## Session timing  (30 min per participant)

*2 people = ~60 min (includes round 2). 3 people = ~90 min. Commentary scales with group size — roughly 2 min per person.*

| Time | Activity | Note |
|------|----------|------|
| **4 min**  | **Scene study**             | Replay the sequence; find its start & end; shout out what resonates and why |
| **6 min**  | **1st pass — sketch 5 shots** | Identify 5 shots, not 5 panels. A shot can span multiple panels |
| **2 min**  | **Catch your breath**        | Decompress; optionally add notes on anything you missed. No structured prompt |
| **5 min**  | **2nd pass — refine**        | Same shots, more resolved. Strengthen the weakest. Use a fresh sheet so you can see your 1st pass |
| **5 min**  | **3rd pass — focus**         | Direct the eye inside each panel — shadow, line weight, contrast |
| **~2 min / pp** | **Commentary track**    | Show-and-tell using the lenses below — pick one or two |
| **— —**    | **Round 2**                 | Next participant's chosen sequence |

> **Peer-to-peer (~30 sec, between 2nd and 3rd pass):** glance at your partner's pages. Steal what inspires you, notice what you missed, feed it into the 3rd pass.

## Scene study  (4 min)

Whoever brought a sequence plays it for the group. If more than one person has something ready, pick by volunteer or at random. Watch it together — once or twice.

**While you watch, shout out what resonates and why.** Don't take structured turns — pile on, let it breathe. Why is this sequence cool? Why did the person who picked it pick it?

**Remember the shots through emotion, not detail.** You're going to draw this from memory. Don't try to memorize content — memorize the **composition**: where the simple shapes sit in the frame, how they layer, and how each shot relates to the one before and after it. The strong emotional beat is the hook that makes the composition stick.

*Find where the sequence starts and ends. That's your frame.*

---

## 1st pass storyboarding  (6 min)

Draw the sequence from memory. **Identify the 5 shots first** — then allocate panels to each. Aim for ~30 seconds per panel; if a shot needs 2 or 3 panels to read, give them. If you finish early, move on; if you're stuck, draw shapes and keep going.

*You're not illustrating — you're reverse-engineering the sequence. Rough is right.*

---

## Catch your breath  (2 min)

Decompress. Stretch. Look out the window. The 1st pass already encoded most of what you saw — you don't need to translate it into words.

*If a panel is bothering you and you want to scribble a note before the 2nd pass, do it. Otherwise, this is a real break — recover and get ready to refine.*

---

## 2nd pass storyboarding  (5 min)

Same shots, more resolved. Add detail to what's working. Sketch quick supplementary panels in the margins if a shot needs more room to land. Strengthen the weakest panel.

**Use a fresh sheet** (or the next page in your notebook). Don't draw on top of the 1st pass — work alongside it so you can see what you're refining against.

*Footnote — "pass" is the standard storyboard workflow: a board gets refined in passes, not finished in one shot.*

---

## 3rd pass storyboarding  (5 min)

The **focus pass**. Now you're directing the viewer's eye inside every panel — the way William Simpson darkens a line to pull you to a character's face in Game of Thrones, or Miyazaki uses shadow and lighting to land the emotional beat of an ekonte panel.

- Where should the eye land first? Push contrast there.
- What should fade back? Soften line weight, simplify shapes.
- Where should the eye travel to next? Set up a visual path.

*You have ~30 seconds per panel. You can't fix everything — pick the move that changes the shot the most.*

---

## Commentary track  (~2 min per person)

Show-and-tell your sequence — not individual shots, the sequence **as a whole**. Pick one or two lenses below and talk your version through using them. Keep this page visible while you speak — it's your cue sheet.

| | Lens | Question |
|---|------|----------|
| ◆ | **Screen direction**     | Which way do characters/motion move? When direction flips, what's the effect? |
| ◆ | **Match cuts & eyeline** | Where does the cut join — on a motion, a look, a shape? What carries through? |
| ◆ | **Pacing & rhythm**      | Which shots are fast, which linger? What's the rhythm of the cut? |
| ◆ | **Emotion continuity**   | What feeling passes from shot to shot? Where does it escalate or break? |
| ◆ | **Action carry**         | Does movement continue across the cut, or contrast against it? |
| ◆ | **Shot duration**        | How long does each shot hold? Which linger, which snap past — why? |
| ◆ | **Camera move**          | Does the camera push in, pull out, pan, hold still? What does the move earn? |
| ◆ | **Scale & distance**     | Wide / medium / close-up — when does the sequence pull in or out, and why then? |
| ◆ | **Focal point shift**    | Where does your eye land in each shot? How does the focal point move across the cut? |

*Homework before next session — watch how pros break down sequences. Director commentary tracks, BTS breakdowns, storyboard artist interviews. Feed the vocabulary back into this list.*
