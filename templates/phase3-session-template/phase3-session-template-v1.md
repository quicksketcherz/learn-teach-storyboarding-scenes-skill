## Before you start

Have a **3-beat script** ready before the timer starts. Two ways to get one:

- **Cold mode — fresh AI-generated script.** Open the scene generator (3-beat mode). Generate. Done. No prior context — this is a script you've never seen before, and neither has your partner.
- **Continuation mode — from a previous Phase 2 round.** Photograph your finished Phase 2 panels. Hand them to Claude (or the scene generator) and ask for a **prequel** *(what led to this?)* or a **sequel** *(what happens next?)*. The AI reads your panels and writes a 3-beat script that connects to them.

*Mix the modes round-to-round. Continuation mode also stress-tests your Phase 2 work — if the AI can't read what's happening in your panels, neither can a future viewer.*

**Drawing surface — choose one:**

- **Option A — Ghibli template, 3 panels used.** Print the same Ghibli 5-panel sheet from Phase 2 — fill 3 of the 5 panels. Leftover panels are scratch space for alt framings or margin sketches.

## The idea

Phase 1 trained your eye on the **frame**. Phase 2 trained it on the **cut**. Phase 3 trains the move that makes someone a storyboard artist: **translating a script into shots**.

There is no source sequence to reverse-engineer. There is no "right answer" hidden in a film you can compare against. There is a script — three beats of action — and the choices are yours. Where's the camera? What's in frame? What's the cut between shot 1 and shot 2? Why this and not something else?

You're not inventing a story. The story is given. You're **directing what we see**.

### From script to storyboards (3 shots)

A script lands on your desk. Three shots, your call.

That's the exercise. The script is the brief; the shots are the deliverable. Same way a real storyboard artist gets a few lines from a director and turns them into images that tell a crew exactly where the camera goes.

*The script is the constraint. The staging is yours.*

---

## Sequence, scene, shot — a quick reminder

| Shot | One unbroken camera take. The smallest unit. |
|------|------------------------------------------------|
| Beat | One narrative move — a thing that happens. |
| Sequence | A chain of beats telling one chunk of story. |

*Phase 3's script is 3 beats. You decide whether each beat is one shot, two shots, or — rarely — folded into a single shot with another beat. The default is one beat per shot; deviations should be deliberate.*

## Session timing  (30 min per participant)

*2 people = ~60 min (includes round 2). Commentary scales with group size — roughly 2 min per person.*

| Time | Activity | Note |
|------|----------|------|
| **4 min**  | **Script read + shot decision**   | Read the 3-beat script. Decide what the 3 shots ARE before you draw |
| **6 min**  | **1st pass — sketch 3 shots**     | Rough thumbnails. Camera, framing, staging. No detail |
| **2 min**  | **Play**                          | Let the mind wander. Anything but the script |
| **5 min**  | **2nd pass — resolve**            | Strengthen the staging. Fix the weakest shot. Fresh sheet |
| **5 min**  | **3rd pass — focus**              | Direct the eye inside each shot — shadow, line weight, contrast |
| **~2 min / pp** | **Commentary track**         | Show-and-tell. Why these 3 shots? Why this cut? |
| **— —**    | **Round 2**                       | Next participant's script (cold or continuation) |

> **Peer-to-peer (~30 sec, between 2nd and 3rd pass):** glance at your partner's pages. Notice where their staging diverged from yours. Feed it into the 3rd pass.

## Script read + shot decision  (4 min)

Read the script twice. The first read is for feeling — what's the tone, what changes between beat 1 and beat 3? The second read is for **decisions**.

Before you draw, decide:

- **What is each shot showing?** Not "what's happening" — that's in the script. *What does the audience see?*
- **Where's the cut?** What's the last frame of shot 1 and the first frame of shot 2? The cut is the move.
- **One beat = one shot, usually.** If a beat needs two shots to read (a reveal, a reaction), give it two. If two beats can land in one shot (a tracking move, a held composition), fold them. **Default is one-to-one. Deviations should be deliberate.**

*This is the new section. In Phase 2 you used these 4 minutes to study the sequence; in Phase 3 you use them to* decide *the sequence. The decisions you make here are the work.*

---

## 1st pass storyboarding  (6 min)

Draw the 3 shots from your decisions. **Rough is right** — masses, framing, camera position, where characters are in frame. No detail.

Aim for ~2 minutes per shot. If you finish early, sketch an alternate framing in the margin — same shot from a different camera. If you're stuck, draw the shape of what should be in frame and move on.

*You're not illustrating the script. You're staging it. The drawing is the record of a directorial decision.*

---

## Play  (2 min)

Step away from the script. Let the mind wander. Stretch, look out the window, doodle something unrelated, hum a song. The point is **not** to think about the work — the work is encoded in your 1st pass already; you don't need to keep churning on it.

*Be in the present moment. The 2nd pass benefits from a brain that's been somewhere else for two minutes.*

---

## 2nd pass storyboarding  (5 min)

Same 3 shots, more resolved. Strengthen the staging — clearer silhouettes, better camera height, more deliberate framing. Fix the weakest shot first; that's where the read breaks.

**Use a fresh sheet** (or the next page in your notebook). Don't draw on top of the 1st pass — work alongside it so you can see what you're refining against.

*If a shot needs an alt — a different camera angle or framing you want to try — sketch it in the margin. The 2nd pass is where you commit to the staging that's going to land.*

---

## 3rd pass storyboarding  (5 min)

The **focus pass**. You've decided the shots and resolved the staging. Now direct the viewer's eye inside every panel.

- Where should the eye land first? Push contrast there.
- What should fade back? Soften line weight, simplify shapes.
- Where should the eye travel to next — and how does that set up the next cut?

*Phase 3 puts more weight on the focus pass than Phase 2 does. In Phase 2 the original director already made these choices and you were re-reading them. Here, you made the choices — the focus pass is where they become legible to someone else.*

---

## Commentary track  (~2 min per person)

Show-and-tell your 3 shots **as a sequence**. Pick one or two lenses below and walk through your version using them. Keep this page visible — it's your cue sheet.

| | Lens | Question |
|---|------|----------|
| ◆ | **Why this shot, not another?**       | What did you choose to show — and what did you choose *not* to show? |
| ◆ | **What does the cut earn?**           | Between shot 1→2 and 2→3 — what changes across the cut? Why cut here? |
| ◆ | **Camera height & distance**          | You picked it. Defend it. What does the choice communicate? |
| ◆ | **Eye entry & exit**                  | Where does the eye enter each shot? Where does it leave — and does that set up the next shot? |
| ◆ | **What's left out of frame**          | Negative space as a directing choice — what's deliberately unseen? |
| ◆ | **Shot economy**                      | Could this beat be told in fewer shots? More? Why is 3 the right count *here*? |
| ◆ | **Tone match**                        | Does the staging fit the script's tone — or fight it (and is fighting it the right move)? |
| ◆ | **Screen direction**                  | Which way do characters or motion move? When direction flips, what's the effect? |
| ◆ | **Scale & distance shift**            | Wide / medium / close-up — when does the sequence pull in or out, and why then? |

*These lenses are a v1 starting set. Run a few rounds and refine — drop the ones that don't pay off, add ones the real conversation surfaces.*
