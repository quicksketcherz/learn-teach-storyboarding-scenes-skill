## Before you start

Decide who's picking the sequence this round. Bring a **specific sequence** you love — not a whole film or show. The clearer the pick, the less time burned scrubbing for a starting point.

Bring your **sketchbook** too — pad, field notebook, whatever you've already been carrying. Sketches go there, not on this template.

*If two people brought something, volunteer or pick at random. Second person's pick goes in Round 2.*

---

## The idea

**Sketch compositions, not sequences.** Each frame is a study of how a single shot is staged — not a recording of what happens across the clip. You're capturing compositions, not reproducing action.

You're not learning to draw — you're training your eye to make fast decisions about what matters in a frame. The sketch is just the record of that decision. Every round, you bring something that resonated with you personally: a film, a show, an anime, a spot. The discussion is where you find out what you saw that the other person didn't.

---

## Your sketchbook is the journal

Don't print this template to draw on. The thumbnails go in your own book — pad, field notebook, sketchbook, whatever you carry. Over time it becomes a **sequence journal**: a page-by-page record of the films and shows you watched, the way another diarist would jot a sentence about their day.

*This template is the conversation scaffold — timing, prompts, the notes you and your partner take together. The drawings live in your book.*

## Session timing  (2 people = 2 rounds in 30 min)

| Time | Activity | Note |
|------|----------|------|
| **5 min** | **Sketch 4 frames + 1 freestyle** | Frames 1–4: **compositions** from your sequence — what's staged, not what happens. Frame 5: your original move. *(See "Steal four. Make the fifth." below)* |
| **1 min** | **Annotate for a minute** | Write 3 things quickly per frame — anchor / feeling / why |
| **2 min** | **Each person shares** | Per person — describe each frame through a different lens (menu below) |
| **— —** | **Repeat for round 2** | Second person's chosen sequence |

> **During the 5-min sketch:** spend at least 30 seconds glancing at your partner's pages. How are they capturing the same beats? What did they pick that you didn't?

---

## Steal four. Make the fifth.

> *"Good artists copy, great artists steal." — and great artists make their own.*

The first four frames are you learning the choreography. **Frame 5 is you improvising within it** — same music, your move.

Within the same 5-minute timer, sketch an original frame inspired by what you just captured. It could be a continuation of the sequence, a prequel to the first frame, or a riff that belongs in the same world. The constraint is the sequence; the choice is yours.

This is where observation becomes invention — you're thinking like a storyboard artist, generating a key frame on the spot rather than reproducing one.

---

## The annotation minute — what to write

For each frame, note three things quickly. Don't overthink it.

- **Anchor** — what does your eye go to first?
- **Feeling** — one or two words. What does this frame make you feel?
- **Why** — one sentence. How does the staging earn that feeling?

*If your notes drift outside these three, that's fine — they often surface what the prompts didn't. Capture the spark, tidy it later.*

## Commentary lenses — pick one per frame to keep it fresh

*Keep this menu visible while you share — it's your cue sheet. A different lens per frame keeps the conversation hitting fresh angles instead of circling the same point.*

| | Lens | Question |
|---|------|----------|
| ◆ | **Depth read** | What's in FG / MG / BG — how do they layer and separate? |
| ◆ | **Silhouette & shape** | If you squint, what reads first? What's the dominant shape? |
| ◆ | **Eyeline & tension** | Where does the eye enter, travel, and land? |
| ◆ | **Negative space** | What's left empty — and why does it work (or not)? |
| ◆ | **Emotion intent** | What feeling does this frame give you? Does the staging earn it? |
